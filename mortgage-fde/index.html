01 / PREMISE

An FDE's product is a method, not a guess at your roadmap.

I am a credit analyst on a $500MM, 60+ loan CRE portfolio, and I build the automation my own desk runs on. I do not know your internal stack, and this page does not pretend to. What it shows is the thing that transfers: how I walk into a lender, extract a workflow carried in templates, legacy knowledge, and practiced habits, find the wedge, and ship a proof-of-value that survives regulated review.

Everything below uses a composite, fictional mid-size lender and four synthetic borrower files. The method is mine; none of the content is any employer's or client's data.

02 / DAYS 1–4

Discovery: the templates are the SOPs.

At most lenders, much of the process is embedded in legacy knowledge and practiced habit. It lives in the templates people fill in and the muscle memory of whoever has done the job longest. I have run this discovery for real on my own desk, roughly 70 annual credit reviews a year, and then built the payoff: updated borrower documents now land as review-ready packages with covenant and safeguard breaches surfaced, instead of hundreds of pages of tax returns read cover to cover.

01
Collect the artifacts, not the org chart. Every template, workbook, and checklist, plus two completed examples of each. Completed files show what practice really is; templates show what it was supposed to be. The delta is where the process lives.
02
Sit with the operator while they work a real file. Time each step. Note every alt-tab, every re-key, every "I just know to check this." The unwritten checks are the judgment layer; they get documented before anything gets automated.
03
Map the workflow and mark every step. Data assembly, calculation, judgment, or routing. On my desk the split came out 60 to 70 percent mechanical, 30 to 40 percent judgment. That boundary is the automation line.
04
Score the candidates. Impact, risk, integration surface. Volume centers first: the recurring grind touching the same document set every time.
05
Baseline before building. Minutes per file, files per month, error classes found in completed work. Without a baseline there is no ROI story, only a demo.

"The first deployment reviews. It never writes."

A checker that catches real errors in completed work earns the team's trust. A writer that makes one error spends all of it.

"Nothing the system ships can decline a borrower."

Outputs are auto-clear or refer-to-underwriter, always with reason codes. Adverse action belongs to a human. That is a fair-lending posture, not a technical limitation.

04 / DAYS 7–12

The worked POV: architecture, evals, ROI.

The build week. This layer sits downstream of document extraction: extraction reads the documents, this reconciles them into a defensible decision.

Extraction payload
Structured fields plus per-field confidence, from the extraction platform
Validation engine
Deterministic: income triangulation, arithmetic integrity, employer consistency, deposit coverage, fraud signals
Human gate
Any field under the confidence threshold routes to the review queue. Corrections captured as structured data
Decision packet
auto_clear or refer, reason codes, reconciliation, full audit trail. Schema-validated JSON
LOS adapter
Conceptual in this sample. Write-back comes last, after trust is earned

The LLM is deliberately absent from the decision path. Judgment stays human; language models draft prose around verified numbers, never the numbers. Ecosystem note: I know the lender-side workflow cold; I have not shipped against the Encompass API and would close that specific gap in the first weeks.

The eval plan, before the build is trusted

A golden dataset of borrower files with hand-verified expectations: the decision, qualifying income to the cent, the exact flag set, the review-queue size. A grader recomputes everything and gates on exact match; no policy change ships without passing. Reviewer corrections become new golden cases, so the human loop makes the system stricter over time.     06 / DAYS 13–14, AND WEEK ONE AT OCROLUS

    The ten questions I would ask first.

    What I cannot know from outside is where your customers actually hurt. Guessing would be malpractice, so here is what I would ask instead.

    01
    Where do your mortgage customers still fall back to manual review after automated conditioning, and what triggers the fallback?
    02
    What does the referral-rate distribution look like across lenders on the same product, and who are the outliers on each end?
    03
    Which POS and LOS integrations generate the most support load per deployment, and is it setup or drift?
    04
    What is the median time from signed contract to first measured ROI on a mortgage POV, and at which step does it stall?
    05
    When an account churns or stalls after the sale, what did its first 30 days look like compared to your best accounts?
    06
    What do underwriters at your best-adopted customer do differently from the median one?
    07
    Which extracted fields drive the most human review volume, and is that volume priced into the customer's ROI model?
    08
    What field feedback from deployed engineers has actually changed the product roadmap in the last two quarters?
    09
    Where do customers ask for decisioning on top of extraction today, and what do they build themselves when you say no?
    10
    What would make this team call a POV playbook successful: fewer bespoke builds, faster time-to-value, or expansion revenue?
